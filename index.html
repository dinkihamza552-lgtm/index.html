Background

What this research is about

Social media platforms like TikTok have emerged as major arenas for political communication. Yet researchers still lack reliable, scalable methods for classifying political content across large datasets.

This project investigates whether large language models (LLMs) can accurately detect political stance and issue content in TikTok videos — and how AI-generated classifications compare to those produced by human annotators.

As part of this work, 300 TikTok videos were manually annotated for political stance, ideological lean, hashtag usage, and issue category. These 12 creator case studies were built from that dataset to understand how politics shapes strategy and reach.

Key Questions

Do political creators also sell products? How does political content perform vs non-political content?

Are there differences in strategy between pro-Trump and pro-Harris creators?

When can LLMs reliably replicate human judgment on political classification tasks?

Analysis

Key Findings

What these 12 creators reveal about political content strategy and the limits of automated classification.

01
Political content doesn't always mean more reach
For most creators, political videos don't outperform regular content. Only dedicated activists like hervoice_matters and raising_woman_ see strong political engagement. Most sellers see the opposite — politics hurts their reach.
02
Pro-Harris creators have larger audiences overall
hervoice_matters, neil_coleman, and coffeetalkwithloulou all exceed 60K+ followers. Most pro-Trump creators in this sample are smaller and more niche — though some like tessadominiqueeray have grown significantly through non-political content.
03
Sellers use politics very differently
mitali.parmar2 integrates politics into product promotion effectively — her political shirts outperform regular ones. But ushao68 and neil_coleman show that mixing politics with products can actually reduce reach and engagement.
04
Identity vs strategy determines performance
Creators who use politics as pure identity (hervoice_matters, toothless.girly) show different patterns than those mixing it with commerce. The approach — not just the stance — determines how the content performs on the platform.
Implications

What this means for AI classification

These case studies directly inform how we build and evaluate LLM-based political content classifiers.

→

Political stance on TikTok is rarely black and white. Creators mix politics with lifestyle, humor, and commerce in ways that make automated classification challenging — and highlight where human judgment is still essential.

→

Human annotation is essential for capturing subtle political signals — like Trump-themed Christmas ornaments or women's rights shirts — that an LLM might miss without broader context about the creator's identity and pattern of content.

→

The engagement gap between pro-Trump and pro-Harris creators in this sample suggests political ideology may affect content reach in ways worth studying at scale — a hypothesis that LLM-based classification can help test systematically.

→

These case studies will directly inform how we design LLM prompts for political content classification, helping AI understand the full spectrum of political expression on TikTok — from explicit endorsements to subtle lifestyle signaling.
